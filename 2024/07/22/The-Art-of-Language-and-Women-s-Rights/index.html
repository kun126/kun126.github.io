The Art of Language and Women's Rights

Kun Chen
2024-07-22

This weekend, I finally watched “Young Woman and the Sea,” a film about Gertrude Caroline Ederle, the first woman to swim across the English Channel. Released earlier in China and available online in Italy, I’ve been eagerly anticipating its screening in Roman cinemas. As a fan of big screens, feminist topics, sci-fi, and documentaries, I frequently browsed social media for reviews. Ultimately, I watched it on my laptop yesterday. While the film was excellent and highly recommended, this post isn’t a storyline analysis or strict review. Instead, I want to share my thoughts on the art of language that the film inspired in me.

First, let’s discuss the movie title’s translation. The original title comprises two elements: “young woman” and “sea.” Paired with the film poster, it implies that the main character might be a swimmer, someone who swims in the sea, or even excels at it. Interestingly, the mainland Chinese version, 泳者之心 or “The Heart of the Swimmer,” removes the gender focus and emphasizes her occupation and tenacity instead. The term ‘tenacity’ culturally reflects the Chinese use of ‘之’ instead of the more colloquial ‘的’ to express ‘of the’ in a quasi-traditional written form. According to the classic Chinese translation theory of Faithfulness (信), Expressiveness (达), and Elegance (雅), the title “泳者之心” seems to lean too much towards Expressiveness and Elegance, sacrificing Faithfulness by omitting the swimmer’s gender—a critical theme of the movie.

The mainland version uniquely removes gender from the title, as evident when compared to translations in other languages. According to IMDb’s “also known as” section translations from 19 countries/regions align with the original title to varying degrees:

  1. Most Aligned (6 countries): UK, US, Australia, Canada, India, and Mexico use the original English title, “Young Woman and the Sea.”
  2. Direct Translations (8 countries): Poland, Argentina, Brazil, Spain, Germany, Japan, Romania, and Russia directly translate the title into their languages as “Young Woman and the Sea.”
  3. Slight Modifications (1 country): Italy modifies it to “The Girl of the Sea,” implying a stronger connection by changing the relationship from constellation to belonging. This change deepens the connection, suggesting that her identity is closely linked with the sea.
  4. Artistic Translations (4 countries): Hungary, France, mainland China, and Taiwan opt for more creative titles. Hungary’s “Girl on the Back of the Waves,” Taiwan’s “The Girl Chasing Waves,” and France’s “Facing the Sea: The Trudy Ederle Story” retain the basic elements but add movement by replacing “sea” into “waves” or use her actual name to highlight the true story.
