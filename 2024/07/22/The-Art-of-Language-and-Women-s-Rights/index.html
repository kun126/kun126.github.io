The Art of Language and Women's Rights

Kun Chen
2024-07-22

This weekend, I finally watched Young Woman and the Sea, a film I had been interested in for several weeks. It tells the story of Gertrude Caroline Ederle, the first woman to swim across the English Channel. The movie was released earlier in China, but it’s only available online in Italy. As someone who loves big screens, feminism, sci-fi, and documentaries, I was eagerly awaiting its release in Rome’s cinemas and kept checking comments on various social media platforms. Eventually, I ended up watching it on my laptop yesterday. While it was excellent and definitely worth recommending, I won’t be doing a detailed analysis or review of the movie. Instead, I’d like to share my reflections on how it has sparked a range of thoughts about the art of language.

The Removal of Gender and Elegant Translation

First, I would like to talk about the movie title’s translation. The original title comprises two elements: “young woman” and “sea.” Paired with the film poster, it implies that the main character might be a swimmer, someone who swims in the sea, or even excels at it. Interestingly, the mainland Chinese version, 泳者之心 or “The Heart of the Swimmer,” removes the gender focus and emphasizes her occupation and tenacity instead. The term ‘tenacity’ culturally reflects the Chinese use of ‘之’ instead of the more colloquial ‘的’ to express ‘of the’ in a quasi-traditional written form. According to the classic translation theory of Faithfulness (信), Expressiveness (达), and Elegance (雅), the title “泳者之心” seems to lean too much towards Expressiveness and Elegance, sacrificing Faithfulness by omitting the swimmer’s gender—a critical theme of the movie.

The mainland China version is the only one removing gender from the title, as evident when compared to translations in other languages. Here is a summary of 19 countries’/regions’ translations from IMDb’s “also known as” section:

  1. Most Aligned (6 countries): UK, US, Australia, Canada, India, and Mexico use the original English title, “Young Woman and the Sea.”
  2. Direct Translations (8 countries): Poland, Argentina, Brazil, Spain, Germany, Japan, Romania, and Russia directly translate the title into their languages as “Young Woman and the Sea.”
  3. Slight Modifications (1 country): Italy modifies it to “The Girl of the Sea,” implying a stronger connection by changing the relationship from constellation to belonging.
  4. Artistic Translations (4 countries): Hungary, France, mainland China, and Taiwan opt for more creative titles. Hungary’s “Girl on the Back of the Waves,” Taiwan’s “The Girl Chasing Waves,” and France’s “Facing the Sea: The Trudy Ederle Story” all preserve the core elements of the title. However, they either replace “sea” with “waves” to highlight the swimmer’s movement or use the actual name rather than “the girl” to emphasize that the story is based on real events.

In the world of translation, a movie title is considered “successful” or “wise” in China if it incorporates local cultural elements, condensing the direct translation while reflecting both the original and implicit meanings of the movie. However, because Chinese nouns are gender-neutral and there is often an overemphasis on the artistry of language, it is impossible to retain any gender-specific meaning if “woman” is removed from the title.

The Distorted Gender Balance and Melodic Language

This has reminded me of another aspect of language I encountered while learning Italian. The art of language here refers to the sound or musicality in the spoken version. Since Italian is inherently a gendered language, most occupations have either a masculine and a feminine version or a gender-neutral version. In Italian, the word for ‘female lawyer,’ ‘avvocata,’ doesn’t sound pleasant, so they typically use the masculine word ‘avvocato’ for both male and female lawyers. I am not exactly sure why ‘avvocata’ doesn’t sound good—it could be the consecutive use of the vowel ‘a’ or a specific letter combination that seems awkward. However, prioritizing the aesthetics of sound over gender equality is truly surprising to me, especially since female lawyers and female law students here seem quite okay with it.

Another subtle aspect lies in the composition of feminine and masculine words. Most gender-neutral words end in ‘e’, while gendered words usually have the same number of vowels. However, this is not the case for academic roles like student, doctor, and professor. In Italian, although these words end in ‘e’ (studente, dottore, and professore), they are all masculine. The feminine versions are formed by adding ‘ssa’ (studentessa, dottoressa, professoressa), resulting in longer pronunciations due to the extra vowel, thus distinguishing the female forms from the male ones. This subtle distinction can be emphasized in teaching, as teachers often introduce the shorter masculine form and its meaning first, briefly mentioning the feminine form afterward. This creates a male-dominant impression for these academic roles, as the masculine version is strongly tied to their meanings. The feminine forms tend to be viewed as subordinate because they disrupt the ‘balance’ of the original sound by adding an extra vowel at the end.

The Role of Language over Art

Language is a powerful tool that shapes the culture and perspectives of future generations. Therefore, prioritizing artistic considerations over gender inclusivity both in language creation and language use can undermine the hard-won progress in women’s rights towards equal representation. It is crucial to balance creativity with a commitment to equality, ensuring that language reflects and promotes the dignity and worth of all individuals.
